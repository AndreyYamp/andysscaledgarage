<section class="project-stage">
  <h2>Introduction</h2>
  <p>The LaFerrari was chosen for its tension and restraint. It is a shape that already carries enough drama, so the build needed to stay calm, clean, and deliberate rather than overly busy.</p>
  <blockquote>
    <p>The goal was simple: let the silhouette speak first, then let the details arrive slowly.</p>
  </blockquote>
  <p>This page follows that same rhythm. The story starts with preparation, moves through assembly and paint, then closes with a final sequence built around soft reflections and careful detail shots.</p>
</section>

<section class="project-stage">
  <h2>Preparation</h2>
  <p>The first stage focused on surface discipline. Before any color could feel convincing, the body needed clear shut lines, quiet edges, and a clean overall flow.</p>
  <!-- Add 2-5 preparation photos here: body shell cleanup, panel lines, dry fit, primer checks. -->
  <ul>
    <li>Body lines were cleaned and panel lines refreshed where the light needed more clarity.</li>
    <li>Key parts were dry-fitted early so the nose, canopy line, and rear deck stayed balanced.</li>
    <li>Reference images were used to judge stance and reflections, not only part placement.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section class="project-stage">
  <h2>Assembly</h2>
  <p>Assembly stayed measured and quiet. The aim was not to show every step, but to keep the structure true enough that the final car would feel effortless.</p>
  <!-- Add 2-5 assembly photos here: chassis, interior tub, suspension mock-up, body test fit. -->
  <ul>
    <li>The chassis and interior were built with fit and alignment taking priority over extra complexity.</li>
    <li>Major joins were checked from low angles to make sure the car would sit with the right sense of weight.</li>
    <li>Hidden areas were kept tidy so the visible surfaces could remain the focus later on.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section class="project-stage">
  <h2>Painting</h2>
  <p>The paint stage was about depth more than brightness. A car like this works best when the red feels rich and settled, with enough gloss to carry the bodywork without turning every surface into glare.</p>
  <!-- Add 2-5 painting photos here: primer, color coats, clear coat, curing, polished body. -->
  <ul>
    <li>Primer was treated as a final inspection step, not just a layer before color.</li>
    <li>The red finish was built patiently so the body kept its shape under the gloss.</li>
    <li>Polishing stayed controlled, with the goal of soft depth rather than a hard mirror effect.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section class="project-stage">
  <h2>Detailing</h2>
  <p>Once the color had settled, the small contrasts began to matter more: dark trim against red paint, carbon textures against smooth panels, and metallic accents used with restraint.</p>
  <!-- Add 2-5 detailing photos here: cockpit, wheel close-up, trim, carbon work, lighting details. -->
  <ul>
    <li>Carbon areas were approached as tonal contrast rather than visual noise.</li>
    <li>Interior details were kept crisp so close shots could feel intimate without becoming cluttered.</li>
    <li>Bright accents were limited to the places where they sharpened the composition.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section class="project-stage">
  <h2>Final Touches</h2>
  <p>The closing work was mostly subtraction: checking reflections, softening anything that looked too harsh, and making sure the finished model photographed with the same calm tone as the rest of the page.</p>
  <!-- Add 2-5 final touch photos here: final assembly, stance check, reflections, cleaned final body. -->
  <ul>
    <li>Panel lines and glass edges were reviewed one last time under soft light.</li>
    <li>The stance was adjusted until the car felt planted without looking forced.</li>
    <li>Dust, fingerprints, and overly bright highlights were removed before the final shoot.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section class="project-stage">
  <h2>Reference Notes</h2>
  <p>The real car was used less as a checklist and more as a guide for mood. The most useful references were the ones that showed how light traveled across the front fenders, how dark elements framed the body, and how little extra drama the shape actually needed.</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Focus on the contrast between painted bodywork and dark trim.</li>
    <li>Keep reflections soft enough that the curves remain readable.</li>
    <li>Favor detail shots that show texture and transitions, not only the full silhouette.</li>
  </ul>
</section>
